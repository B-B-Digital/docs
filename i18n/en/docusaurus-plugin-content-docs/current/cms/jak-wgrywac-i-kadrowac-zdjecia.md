# How do I upload and crop photos?

An image field in the panel looks unassuming — a rectangle that says "Upload image," or one that already has a thumbnail in it. Behind it, though, are two things worth knowing before you upload your first photo: cropping, and alt text.

## Uploading

Click the image field, pick a file from your computer or drag one in — that's it. The photo lands straight in your site's media library and is ready to use.

## Cropping and the focal point

The same photo can end up used in more than one place on the site — once as a wide banner, once as a square thumbnail in a list. The panel doesn't ask you to prepare a separate file for each of those layouts. Instead, you mark a focal point on the photo, for example a face in a portrait, and the site makes sure that point stays visible whenever the photo is cropped, whether the frame is wide or narrow. Think of it like sliding a window frame across a bigger photograph: what shows through changes with the shape of the frame, but the point of interest always stays inside it.

## Alt text

Next to almost every photo, you'll also find a field called "Alt text." It's a short description of what's in the picture — for example, "Dr. Anna Kowalska in the dental office." Fill it in every time it appears, for two reasons. First, Google reads it when indexing images. Second, people using a screen reader — because they're blind or have low vision — will hear that description instead of the photo itself. For you, it's one sentence. For some visitors, it's the only way to know what the photo shows at all.

## What shape of photo to prepare

Team member photos work best in a portrait orientation, similar to a passport photo, just with more room around the person. Other photos on the site — the homepage background, a service photo, a blog cover — don't have one fixed shape, so you can go with whatever simply looks good in that spot.

## Before you upload

A phone can easily produce a photo weighing several megabytes, and the site doesn't need that much resolution to look sharp on screen. Before uploading, it's worth shrinking the file — down to a few hundred kilobytes, or at most 1–2 megabytes — using any free online image compression tool. Also make sure the photo has the right orientation (not sideways) and that the frame actually shows what you care about, before you start cropping it in the panel. A few seconds of preparation before uploading saves time on every crop afterward.
